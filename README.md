# Ethical Dilemmas in AI Recruitment Software: An Empirical Study of Crossover's AI Chatbot Recruiter

**Module:** Explainable and Ethical AI — PGDip Artificial Intelligence (Human Centred)  
**Institution:** Bournemouth University, UK  
**Grade:** Near First Class 
**Year:** 2025  

---

## Overview

This original empirical research study investigates the ethical implications of Crossover's AI-powered recruitment chatbot — a system that replaces the traditional CV, cover letter, and human interview with an automated one-hour chatbot interaction.

The study was designed in direct response to the UK Government's Department for Science, Innovation and Technology (DSIT) 2024 guidance on responsible AI deployment in recruitment, which challenged organisations to ask: does AI recruitment create new barriers for protected groups? Does it amplify existing biases at scale?

This research answers those questions through original qualitative interviews with members of the public — and through an innovative comparative methodology: asking the same research questions to two frontier GenAI systems (Gemini and ChatGPT) and comparing their responses to human responses.

---

## Research Questions

1. What do lay people think of an automated recruitment process with no CV or cover letter — only a chatbot?
2. Who benefits from an AI chat recruiter, and how?
3. Who is disadvantaged by an AI chat recruiter, and how?

---

## Methodology

### Phase 1: Qualitative Interviews with Members of the Public
- **5 participants** recruited from diverse backgrounds: a therapist, a mental health nurse, a philosophy/political science graduate, an undergraduate illustration student, and a civil engineering student
- Participants watched a real video recording of a job applicant completing Crossover's chatbot recruitment process from start to finish
- Semi-structured interviews conducted after viewing, exploring perceptions of fairness, bias, and impact on vulnerable groups
- **Thematic analysis** applied across 8 themes

### Phase 2: GenAI Comparative Analysis (Novel Methodology)
- The identical research questions were posed to **Gemini** and **ChatGPT**
- AI responses were systematically compared to human responses
- Divergences and blind spots in both human and AI reasoning were documented and analysed
- This methodology surfaces what AI systems can and cannot perceive about their own societal impact

---

## Thematic Analysis Results

| Theme | Finding |
|-------|---------|
| Absence of CV & Cover Letter | 80% of participants surprised a chatbot could replace both |
| Crossover's Gains | 100% agreed the organisation benefits from the system |
| Applicant Benefits | 60% agreed neurodiverse candidates may benefit — less discrimination risk |
| Applicant Disadvantages | 40% believed applicants may be materially disadvantaged |
| AI Should Not Replace Traditional Process | 60% felt AI should never fully replace human recruitment |
| Explainability Concerns | 40% questioned the chatbot's decision-making process |
| AI Prejudice Risk | 40% concerned about bias; 20% felt bias was inevitable |
| Cheating Risk | 20% strongly felt the chatbot enables identity fraud and GenAI cheating |

---

## Key Findings

### Human Interview Insights
- Neurodiverse candidates who struggle with in-person interviews may benefit significantly
- Traditional interview advantages (body language, rapport, storytelling) are entirely absent
- No mechanism exists to verify candidate identity or detect GenAI-assisted responses
- Time pressure (one-hour limit) disproportionately disadvantages candidates with certain mental health conditions
- Loss of cover letter removes applicants' ability to contextualise non-traditional career paths

### GenAI Comparative Analysis
- GenAI identified more disadvantaged groups than human participants (e.g. non-traditional background candidates, career changers)
- **Critical blind spot:** GenAI failed to identify that traditional human interviews perpetuate racial discrimination — and that AI chatbots may inadvertently reduce this. This insight emerged only from human participant responses.
- GenAI correctly identified that nuance and contextual understanding remain superior in human interviewers
- Both GenAI systems and human participants agreed: organisations are the primary beneficiary of AI recruitment

### Legal & Regulatory Dimension
- GDPR Article 22 prohibits fully automated decision-making in hiring — Crossover has a legal obligation to ensure human oversight at the final decision stage
- The EU AI Act classifies AI recruitment systems as high-risk — requiring transparency, human oversight, and bias auditing
- The Law of Unintended Consequences: filtering out candidates who don't match the algorithm's criteria may eliminate exactly the diverse, non-traditional talent organisations most need

---

## Significance

This study makes three original contributions:

1. **Empirical data** from lay people (not AI experts) on the perceived ethics of AI recruitment — a perspective largely absent from the academic literature
2. **A novel comparative methodology** using GenAI as a research subject alongside humans — demonstrating both the capabilities and blind spots of frontier AI systems when reasoning about their own societal impact
3. **Policy-relevant conclusions** aligned with DSIT guidance, GDPR Article 22, and EU AI Act — directly relevant to organisations deploying AI in high-stakes human decisions
